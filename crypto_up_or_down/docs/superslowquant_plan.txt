1. Model Structure

Price evolves as:

dS/S = μ dt + σ dW + J dN

Where:

Term	Meaning
μ	drift
σ	diffusion volatility
dW	Brownian motion
dN	Poisson jump process
J	jump size

Meaning:

Most of the time price moves normally:

σ dW

But occasionally:

jump event

This models:

liquidations

ETF news

macro shocks

exchange failures

Which matter a lot for threshold probabilities.

2. Jump Process

Jump arrivals follow:

N ~ Poisson(λT)

Where:

λ = jump frequency

Example crypto parameters:

Parameter	Typical
λ	0.2–0.4 per week
jump mean	±8%
jump std	6–10%
3. Jump Size Distribution

Usually:

log(J) ~ Normal(μj, σj)

Example:

μj = 0
σj = 0.08

Meaning jumps are around:

±8%
4. Why This Matters for Polymarket

Consider market:

BTC > 90k in 10 days

Black-Scholes might say:

4%

Jump diffusion might say:

9%

Because one +15% jump makes it possible.

Retail traders systematically underprice this.

5. Monte Carlo Probability Calculation

Instead of closed-form equations, desks simulate paths.

Algorithm:

simulate N price paths
count how many finish above strike
Pseudocode
for i in range(N):

    price = S0

    for t in time_steps:

        diffusion_move = sigma * sqrt(dt) * normal()

        if random() < lambda * dt:
            jump = normal(mu_j, sigma_j)
        else:
            jump = 0

        price *= exp((mu - 0.5*sigma^2)*dt + diffusion_move + jump)

    if price > strike:
        success += 1

Probability:

P = success / N

1. Building a Probability Curve from Multiple Strike Markets

When Polymarket lists markets like:

BTC > 70k by June
BTC > 75k by June
BTC > 80k by June
BTC > 85k by June
BTC > 90k by June

Each market gives you:

P(S_T > K)

Where:

K = strike

This is called the survival probability function.

A professional desk will convert these prices into a full probability distribution of BTC at expiry.

Step 1 — Collect Market Prices

Example:

Strike	YES Price
70k	0.82
75k	0.68
80k	0.54
85k	0.39
90k	0.27

These represent:

P(S_T > K)
Step 2 — Convert to Interval Probabilities

Probability BTC ends between two strikes:

P(K1 < S_T < K2)

Computed as:

P(S_T > K1) − P(S_T > K2)

Example:

P(75k–80k)
= 0.68 − 0.54
= 0.14

Full table:

Range	Probability
<70k	0.18
70–75k	0.14
75–80k	0.14
80–85k	0.15
85–90k	0.12

90k | 0.27 |

This is essentially the market-implied distribution.

Step 3 — Build the Market Probability Curve

Plot:

strike vs survival probability

Which looks like a downward sloping curve.

Professionally this is called:

the implied CDF

CDF = cumulative distribution function.

Step 4 — Convert to Probability Density

The probability density is:

pdf(K) = − d/dK P(S_T > K)

In practice:

difference between adjacent strikes

This tells you where the market thinks price will land.

Step 5 — Compare to Your Model

Your model generates its own probabilities:

P_model(S_T > K)

You now compare:

market_curve vs model_curve

Where they diverge = trading opportunities.
